Nobody agrees what 'intelligence' means, but there's still an interesting answer to this question.

Robert Freitas came up with something called the Sentience quotient (SQ) in the late ‘70s, which expresses the information-processing efficiency of a brain, independent of its internal makeup. Here 'brain' is to be broadly construed to mean 'information-processing unit'.

The formula is very simple: it's just the base-10 log of the ratio of the brain's information-processing rate (in bits/sec) to the brain's mass (in kilograms). (For more, I encourage you to read his highly readable paper here: Xenopsychology. The following is mostly a summary of the paper's last section.)

To get a handle on the range of SQs we encounter in nature, it's instructive to calculate them for a few familiar organisms. Human neurons have an average mass of ∼100∼100nanograms and can process information at 1000-3000 bits/sec (according to Freitas), so this gives us an SQ of 13. It turns out that all animals with nervous systems, from insects to mammals, cluster within several points of this value. Plants cluster around an SQ of -2, fully 15 points below humans in infoproc efficiency, or a million billion times lower if you're keeping track of your logarithms; interestingly, carnivorous plants like the Venus flytrap have SQs around 1 (because their response times are in the seconds regime, rather than hours). Computers fare pretty well — IBM Watson for instance has an SQ of 11-12 — and have steadily gotten better over time; they'll probably catch up with us within the next few years if they haven't already.

What's the lowest possible SQ? We can imagine a hypothetical 'brain' comprising the mass of the entire observable universe, which takes the universe's age to process a single bit of information: that results in an SQ of -70.

What's the highest possible SQ? Freitas notes that for all information to be acted upon, it must be represented physically and carried by matter-energy 'markers'. The lower limit for the accuracy with which energy can be measured is given by Planck's constant hh divided by the duration of measurement tt. Using one energy level to represent one bit, the maximum possible bit rate will then be given by the total energy E=mc2E=mc2available for representing information, divided by the minimum measurable energy per bit htht, all divided by the minimum time required for readout tt, giving mc2hmc2h, for a maximum possible SQ imposed by physical laws of 50.

Note that this is much further removed from us than we are from plants (let alone animals with nervous systems, let alone animals considered intelligent e.g. dogs or birds). Freitas noted that attempts to communicate with even reasonably intelligent animals separated from us by at most a few SQ points are already inordinately difficult, and not very meaningful beyond say 10 SQ points (he gives the example of communication with plants being limited to planting, watering and fertilizing them).

I don't know about you, but just trying to conceive of an SQ 50 intelligence, or really any intelligence even a few points higher up the SQ ladder than we are, sends chills up my spine.
